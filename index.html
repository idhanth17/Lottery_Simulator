📐 The math under the hood

This is the “psychic modeling” problem from Steven Skiena's Algorithm Design Manual. If a psychic guarantees that at least j of their n chosen numbers will be among the t drawn, then since your tickets only use the psychic's numbers, the only thing that matters is which j of the n come true. The worst case is exactly j, so there are C(n, j) scenarios to defend against, and you win if some ticket shares at least l numbers with the scenario.

Choosing the fewest tickets whose coverage spans every scenario is the classic NP-hard set cover problem. This site runs a greedy approximation (pick the ticket covering the most unprotected scenarios, repeat), randomized restarts, and a redundancy pruner — right in your browser. It also computes the counting lower bound ⌈C(n,j) ⁄ coverage-per-ticket⌉ so you can see how close the answer is to provably optimal, and an independent verifier re-checks every scenario before showing you the tickets.

Skiena's team first modelled this wrongly — covering all C(n,l) triples — and got nearly twice as many tickets. The fix (“cover every j-subset, not every l-subset”) is the whole moral of the war story: model the problem correctly before solving it.
